The key to this puzzle is Bayes' Theorem, which speaks to probabilities (uncertainties) as they are modified with a gain in information.

You are given three doors to choose from, one of which contains a car and the other two contain goats. After you've chosen one but haven't opened it, Monty, who knows where everything is, reveals the location of a goat from behind one of the other two doors. Should you stick with your original choice or switch, if you want the car?

The additional information after the first choice has been made has changed the probabilities. The total probability is still one — the car is certainly behind one of the doors. So, would you keep your original choice, or switch to the other door? Does it make a difference? Justify your choice and submit your result.
🧮 The Math You Need — Bayes' Theorem
ScenarioInitial ProbabilityAfter Monty Reveals GoatResult
Stay with original door1/3 (33.3%)1/3 (33.3%) — unchanged❌ Probability does NOT improve
Switch to other door1/3 (33.3%)2/3 (66.7%) — doubles✅ ALWAYS SWITCH — odds double

Why? Monty's reveal is not random — he always shows a goat. This injects information that shifts the remaining probability to the unchosen door. Your original pick locked in 1/3. The other door absorbs the remaining 2/3 after the reveal.
